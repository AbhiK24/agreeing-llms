# The final act: from measuring ρ to choosing a committee

**Source**: all numbers from [`../raw/responses.pre-final-filter.jsonl.bak`](../raw/responses.pre-final-filter.jsonl.bak)
(the frozen T=0 sweep — every one of the 8 models answered all 750 items, so
every subset of the pool is a valid committee; zero new API calls).
Generated by `python -m rho_collapse.committee_analysis`; full tables in
[`../committee-selection/`](../committee-selection/) (`report.md`,
`committees.csv`, `selection_eval.json`).
This analysis was added after the v1 freeze; it reads frozen raw data and
touches nothing under `raw/`, `dataset/`, or `config/`.

The paper's arc, with the numbers that carry each act:

1. Committees use agreement as confidence → only valid under independence
2. We define ρ and measure it → it's high (N_eff ≈ 1.3–1.8, paper-v1)
3. So *choose* a committee with good accuracy and good ρ → **harder than it
   looks: the naive objective falls into the paper's own competence confound**
4. And whatever you choose **does not generalize across domains** — not the
   winner, not accuracy ranks, not even the ρ structure
5. What survives out-of-sample is a *protocol*, not a roster: floor by
   accuracy, size by N_eff, fill by cost → **beats the industry default in
   every domain at 3–9× lower cost**

---

## Finding C1 — Small committees are all you can buy (enumeration, 219 committees × 3 domains)

The best size-3 committee matches or beats the best committee of ANY larger
size, and the full 8-model pool is *worse* than a good trio:

| Domain | best-3 | best-5 | all-8 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Law | **0.715** | 0.716 | 0.699 |
| Medicine | 0.858 | **0.861** | 0.851 |
| Science | **0.918** | 0.916 | 0.905 |

This is exactly what the design effect predicts: at the measured ρ̄ ≈ 0.5–0.65,
N_eff(3) ≈ 1.5 and N_eff(8) ≈ 1.8 — members 4–8 contribute almost no
independent evidence, but they still contribute correlated *errors* to the
vote and full price to the bill.

## Finding C2 — The naive "minimize ρ" objective is a trap

Within every domain, committee ρ̄ correlates *positively* with plurality
accuracy (Spearman +0.13 to +0.36), because strong models share few errors in
absolute terms but the errors they do make overlap — while weak models look
"diverse" (the competence confound from analysis/02, now biting at selection
time). A corrected-evidence greedy (maximize N_eff · log-odds) picks GLM 4.6
into science committees and loses to plain top-accuracy selection:

- Split-half (20 splits, size 5): ρ-aware greedy 0.680/0.844/0.889 vs
  top-accuracy 0.694/0.848/0.900 (law/medicine/science).
- Evidence-score Spearman vs realized plurality accuracy: −0.19 (science),
  +0.10 (medicine), +0.59 (law). Not an objective you can optimize.

**ρ tells you how much confidence agreement buys and how many members are
worth paying for. It does not tell you which roster wins.**

## Finding C3 — Nothing about the roster transfers across domains

Spearman rank correlation across the 56 size-5 committees:

| Domain pair | accuracy ranks | ρ̄ ranks |
|---|---|---|
| law vs medicine | −0.23 | +0.19 |
| law vs science | +0.38 | −0.30 |
| medicine vs science | −0.46 | **−0.54** |

Deploying one domain's winning committee on another gives up as much as 5.3
points (medicine's winner on law). Even the correlation *structure* is
domain-specific: a pair of models that errs together on medicine does not err
together on science. There is no such thing as a "generally diverse"
committee in this pool — selection must be per-domain, from a small
calibration slice.

Within-domain isn't much friendlier: split-half roster predictability is
domain-dependent (law Spearman 0.61, medicine ≈ 0.0), and no selection
strategy reliably beats top-accuracy. What *is* robust: min-member accuracy
drives committee accuracy (Spearman +0.36 to +0.54) — don't seat weak models.

## Finding C4 — DEFT beats current practice everywhere, at 3–9× lower cost

Since roster fine-tuning doesn't transfer, the defensible algorithm optimizes
only what the data supports. We call it **DEFT — Design-Effect-guided Frugal
Teams** (Algorithm 1 in draft §3.4; reference implementation
`rho_collapse.committee.protocol_select`). Its four steps are the verbs:

```text
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Algorithm 1  DEFT  (compact form; full box in paper/draft.md §3.4)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1 CALIBRATE  run every candidate once on ~100 items → p̂_m, ρ̂_ij
2 FLOOR      Q ← { m : p̂_m ≥ max p̂ − δ }              (δ = 0.10)
3 SIZE       N* ← largest N with N_eff(N,ρ̂) − N_eff(N−1,ρ̂) ≥ ε
             N_eff(N,ρ) = N / (1 + (N−1)ρ)             (ε = 0.10)
4 FILL       S ← N* cheapest members of Q, subject to budget
5 PRICE      decide: plurality answer a*, k votes →
             q = P(correct | k_eff), k_eff = k·N_eff/|S|;
             ACCEPT iff q ≥ τ, else escalate/abstain
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

Each step is licensed by a measured failure of something greedier: FLOOR by
the competence confound (C2), SIZE by committee saturation (C1), FILL by the
cross-domain transfer failure (C3), PRICE by the overconfidence gap
(analysis/03).

Against the industry default (N seeds of one strong model — our D1 cell,
rescored with the same abstention voting for comparability):

| Domain | DEFT committee | Acc | Cost | 5 seeds DeepSeek | Acc | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Law | claude+gemini+gpt5-mini | **0.667** | 1,557 | | 0.585 | 4,665 |
| Medicine | claude+kimi (N=2!) | **0.832** | 297 | | 0.817 | 2,759 |
| Science | claude+gpt5-mini+kimi | **0.891** | 815 | | 0.867 | 3,823 |

(cost = mean output tokens per item, summed over members)

DEFT wins every domain while costing 3×–9× less. Two details worth a
sentence each in the paper:

- **Law, five T=0.7 seeds score 0.585 — below a single T=0 DeepSeek call
  (0.611).** Temperature sampling plus voting is not ensembling: the noise
  hurts each seed, and ρ = 0.755 means voting cannot average it away.
- **Medicine's protocol committee has two members.** At ρ̂ ≈ 0.65 the design
  effect prices the third member below the 0.1-effective-agent threshold —
  the formula that diagnosed overconfidence now makes procurement decisions.

The protocol does *not* beat top-5-accuracy on raw accuracy (0.708/0.859/0.910
— it concedes 2–4 points); it beats it on accuracy-per-token by ~4–11×, and
top-5 requires already knowing all models' accuracies, i.e. the same
calibration slice the protocol needs. On equal information, the protocol is
the one that also right-sizes N and prices the committee.

## Honest caveats for the paper

- Selection and evaluation share the paper-v1 sweep for C1–C3 enumeration
  (in-sample); the out-of-sample claims come from split-half and
  leave-one-domain-out, which is where the transfer failures show up. The
  protocol's head-to-head is in-sample for the floor/fill inputs — v2 should
  re-validate on a held-out benchmark (GPQA-Diamond, already planned).
- 8 models, 3 domains, English MCQ. The transfer-failure finding (C3) in
  particular needs more domains before it's a law rather than an observation.
- Abstention-tolerant plurality over all 250 items/domain differs from
  paper-v1's committee-complete filter (L0); it's the production-realistic
  choice and keeps item sets identical across committees, but it's why
  accuracies here are lower than FINDINGS.md headline numbers.
- ρ̂ from ~125-item calibration slices is noisy; the protocol only consumes
  it through N_eff sizing, which is robust to that noise (the N decision
  changes only when ρ̂ crosses wide thresholds).
